Edge Feature — Technical Design DocumentThe financial markets of the year 2026 demand a level of analytical precision that transcends simple technical indicators or basic statistical summaries. The modern quantitative researcher requires a centralized ecosystem capable of distilling raw time-series data into actionable "edges." This document outlines the technical design for the /edge route, a sophisticated architecture dedicated to realized and implied volatility analysis, cross-asset validation, and market regime detection. By integrating advanced stochastic modeling with a pessimistic trade simulator, this feature set seeks to bridge the gap between "interesting data" and "decision-ready alpha."The philosophy of the Edge feature is grounded in the triad of accessibility, professionalism, and mathematical rigor. For the novice user, the system provides an intuitive "layman’s" explanation of market dynamics—analogous to weather forecasting for financial storms. For the professional analyst, it offers a refined technical framework for risk management and strategy optimization. For the institutional-grade researcher, it provides a comprehensive reference layer complete with peer-reviewed mathematical formulations and empirical citations. This multi-layered approach ensures that the /edge feature is both a pedagogical tool for growth and a rigorous environment for production-grade quantitative finance.1. Quantitative Estimation of Realized VolatilityRealized volatility is the foundational metric for understanding how an asset has moved over a historical window. While the concept is intuitively simple, its calculation is fraught with statistical nuances that can significantly impact the accuracy of a trading model.1.1 Layman’s Explanation: Measuring the Market’s PulseTo a casual observer, volatility is simply a measure of how "choppy" or "jumpy" a stock’s price has been. If a stock moves 5% up and down every day, it is highly volatile; if it moves only 0.1%, it is calm. We calculate this pulse to understand the risk of a position. Imagine you are driving a car; volatility is the measurement of how much you are shaking the steering wheel. Even if you are traveling in a straight line (a trending market), high volatility means you are making large corrections, which increases the chance of an accident.1.2 Professional Analysis: The Annualized Standard DeviationFor the professional trader, realized volatility is defined as the annualized standard deviation of logarithmic returns. Unlike simple percentage changes, log returns are additive and more appropriate for statistical modeling. Volatility is not a constant; it clusters in periods of high and low intensity. Professionals use realized volatility to size their positions—reducing exposure when the "pulse" of the market accelerates to keep the total dollar-risk of the portfolio constant. This process, known as volatility targeting, is a staple of modern risk management.1.3 Comprehensive Reference: Statistical Estimators and EfficiencyThe Edge feature implements four primary estimators to provide a comprehensive view of historical movement, each with varying levels of efficiency and bias. Efficiency, in this context, refers to how much information an estimator extracts from a given set of data points compared to the standard close-to-close method.EstimatorData RequirementsStatistical PropertyEfficiency GainClose-to-CloseClosing PricesStandard, but ignores intraday data1.0x (Baseline)Parkinson (1980)High, LowCaptures intraday range ~5.0xGarman-Klass (1980)Open, High, Low, CloseBest for zero-drift assumptions ~7.4xYang-Zhang (2000)OHLC + Previous CloseUnbiased for opening gaps and drift ~14.0xThe Yang-Zhang estimator is the preferred choice for the Edge feature because it addresses the "opening jump" problem. In modern markets, a significant portion of volatility occurs overnight when the exchange is closed. Standard range-based estimators like Parkinson or Garman-Klass fail to account for this gap, leading to an underestimation of true risk. The Yang-Zhang formula ($\sigma_{YZ}$) combines the overnight variance, the open-to-close variance, and the Rogers-Satchell variance to create a measure that is both drift-independent and consistent.$$\sigma_{YZ}^2 = \sigma_{overnight}^2 + k \sigma_{open-to-close}^2 + (1-k) \sigma_{RS}^2$$where $k$ is chosen to minimize the variance of the estimator, defined as $k = 0.34 / (1.34 + \frac{N+1}{N-1})$. By utilizing the Yang-Zhang method, the Edge feature provides a measurement that is up to 14 times more efficient than simple close-to-close calculations for small sample sizes. This efficiency allows for faster detection of regime shifts with fewer data points.Furthermore, for intraday analysis at 15-minute intervals, the system incorporates Realized Kernel (RK) estimators. Developed by Barndorff-Nielsen et al. (2008), these kernels are designed to be robust against "microstructure noise"—the artificial volatility created by the bid-ask bounce and irregular trade timing. By applying a Parzen kernel weight to the empirical autocovariances of high-frequency returns, the RK estimator provides a "clean" measure of the underlying efficient price process.2. Implied Volatility and the Variance Risk PremiumIf realized volatility is a record of the past, implied volatility (IV) is the market’s forecast of the future. The Edge feature places the relationship between these two metrics at the center of its analytical framework.2.1 Layman’s Explanation: The Price of ProtectionThink of implied volatility as the cost of an insurance premium. If everyone expects a hurricane, the cost of flood insurance goes up, even if it hasn’t started raining yet. In the stock market, when investors are scared, they buy "put options" to protect their portfolios, driving up the IV. The "Variance Risk Premium" (VRP) is the difference between what people paid for insurance (IV) and what the storm actually did (Realized Volatility). Usually, people overpay for insurance out of fear, and the VRP is positive. Selling options is essentially acting as the insurance company, collecting that premium.2.2 Professional Analysis: Forward Expectations vs. Ex-Post RealizationProfessionals view the VRP as a compensated risk factor. It is the spread earned by providing liquidity and tail-risk protection to the market. The Edge system calculates the forward ex-post VRP by comparing the IV at time $t$ for a 30-day tenor against the volatility that actually realized over the subsequent 30 days.$$\widehat{VRP}_t^{(30d)} = (IV_{t}^{(30d)})^2 - (RV_{t \to t+30d})^2$$A persistent positive VRP indicates that the market is consistently overpricing risk, suggesting a profitable environment for short-volatility strategies like straddle selling or iron condors. Conversely, a negative VRP suggests that the market is underestimating upcoming movement, making long-volatility positions more attractive.2.3 Comprehensive Reference: Term Structure and Surface InversionTo ensure high-fidelity IV data, the Edge feature does not rely on a single at-the-money (ATM) quote. Instead, it processes the entire volatility surface through a delta-based moneyness inversion process.SABR/SVI Surface Query: The system queries the existing volatility/surface.py module to retrieve the volatility smile for a given expiry.Fixed-Point Inversion: To find the strike price $K$ corresponding to a specific delta $\Delta$ (e.g., 25-delta put), the system employs a Newton-Raphson solver to invert the Black-Scholes delta formula.$$K = S \exp\left$$Because $\sigma$ varies with $K$, the solver iterates through the surface until convergence is reached.VIX-Style Interpolation: The system constructs a 30-day constant maturity IV30 by interpolating in variance space between the two nearest option expiries, following the CBOE VIX methodology.$$\sigma_{30}^2 = \frac{1}{T_{30}} \left$$This interpolation is critical for curing "calendar bias." Options expire on fixed dates, but the Edge Score requires a consistent 30-day forecast. By weighting the expiries correctly, the system provides a smooth, continuous signal that reflects the true term structure of fear.3. Data Isolation and Anti-Leakage ArchitectureThe most common failure in quantitative research is the "oracle" or "look-ahead" bias. This occurs when a model inadvertently uses information from the future to make a prediction about the past.3.1 Layman’s Explanation: No Peeking at the AnswersImagine you are taking a math test, and the answers to tomorrow's test are accidentally printed on the back of today's paper. If you use those answers, you’ll get an A, but you won't actually know any math. In trading, if your computer "peeks" at tomorrow's stock price to decide whether to buy today, your results will look amazing in your simulation, but you will lose money in real life because you won't have that "peek" at the future.3.2 Professional Analysis: Structural Enforcement of Temporal IntegrityThe Edge feature implements a "Hard Split" architecture to prevent data contamination. Features—the inputs used for prediction—are physically separated from Labels—the outcomes used for evaluation.LayerDirectoryTemporal RuleReal-time Featuresfeatures_realtime/Only $t \le now$. Every code commit to the volatility branch is scanned by a pytest hook that uses regular expressions to detect forbidden patterns.Forbidden Shifts: Any instance of .shift(-N) within the features_realtime/ directory triggers an immediate build failure.Forbidden Imports: The CI guard blocks any code in the feature layer from importing modules located in the labels_oracle/ layer.Furthermore, the system adopts a "Canonical Timestamp Rigor" [numerical-rigor.md]. All data points are stored as 64-bit integer milliseconds in UTC. This avoids the common pitfalls of timezone transitions and daylight savings time, which can create artificial "gaps" or "overlaps" in time-series data. When calculating intraday volatility at a 15-minute resolution, even a one-hour offset due to a DST error can completely invalidate the alignment of IV and RV, leading to a false VRP signal.4. Market Regime Clustering and State-Space TransitionsFinancial markets are not stationary; they transition between different "regimes" characterized by varying levels of trend, volatility, and liquidity.4.1 Layman’s Explanation: The Four Seasons of the MarketJust as you wouldn't plant seeds in the winter or go skiing in the summer, you shouldn't use a "trend-following" strategy when the market is just bouncing up and down in a range. Regimes are the "seasons" of the stock market. Some months are "Sunny" (low volatility, steady growth), while others are "Stormy" (high volatility, sharp drops). Our job is to build a "weather station" that tells us which season we are in so we can pick the right tools for the job.4.2 Professional Analysis: Unsupervised Learning for State DetectionThe Edge feature uses unsupervised machine learning to cluster market data into distinct regimes. By analyzing features such as the trend slope, Yang-Zhang volatility, and the 25-delta skew, the system can identify when the market’s "hidden state" has shifted from a low-volatility bullish regime to a high-volatility sideways regime. This allows the Edge Score to adjust its recommendations dynamically—for instance, by favoring "mean-reversion" strategies during choppy regimes and "breakout" strategies during trending regimes.4.3 Comprehensive Reference: Hidden Markov Models (HMM) and HSMMThe core algorithm for regime detection is the Hidden Markov Model (HMM), specifically the Gaussian HMM. Unlike simple clustering (K-means), which treats each day as an independent event, the HMM recognizes that today’s market state is highly likely to be the same as yesterday’s.The HMM is defined by:Hidden States ($z_t$): The unobserved regime (e.g., State 1, 2, or 3).Transition Matrix ($A$): The probability of moving from state $i$ to state $j$.Emission Distribution: The probability of observing a specific set of features (OHLCV, IV, etc.) given the hidden state $z_t$.For advanced users, the system provides a Hidden Semi-Markov Model (HSMM) option. While a standard HMM assumes a geometric distribution for the time spent in each state, the HSMM allows for explicit "duration modeling". This is particularly useful in finance because market regimes tend to be "sticky"—a bull market might last for years, while a crash regime might last for only a few weeks. The HSMM better captures this temporal asymmetry, leading to fewer "false alarms" and a more stable regime signal.FeatureK-MeansHMMHSMMTemporal AwarenessNoYesYesState PersistenceLow (High Turnover)ModerateHigh (Duration Modeled)Computational CostLowModerateHighBest ForSimple groupingShort-term signalsLong-term regimes To maintain consistency across monthly refits of the model, the system uses the Hungarian Algorithm for label alignment. This optimization ensures that "State 0" always represents the low-volatility regime, even as new data is added to the training set, preventing the "label switching" problem that often plagues machine learning pipelines.5. Cross-Asset Validation and Robustness MetricsA strategy that works on a single stock may be a statistical fluke. To find a true "edge," a signal must demonstrate robustness across multiple assets and time periods.5.1 Layman’s Explanation: The "Not a One-Hit Wonder" TestIf a singer has one hit song but can't ever write another, they are a one-hit wonder. In trading, if your "buy" signal works on Apple but loses money on Microsoft, Google, and the S&P 500, then your signal was probably just a lucky coincidence. We test our signals across a whole group of different stocks (SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA) to make sure they are "rock stars," not just lucky one-timers.5.2 Professional Analysis: Multi-Asset Aggregation and SplittingThe Cross-Asset Validation view automates the testing of a signal across the "Major Four" ETFs: SPY (S&P 500), QQQ (Nasdaq 100), IWM (Russell 2000), and DIA (Dow Jones).The system supports three types of validation splits:Rolling Windows: Testing the strategy in N-year increments to see how it performs in different eras.Calendar Years: Identifying specific years where the strategy failed (e.g., 2022's bear market).Walk-Forward Optimization: An anchored train-test split that simulates how a trader would have actually updated their model over time.5.3 Comprehensive Reference: Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) and PBOTo provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for robustness, the Edge feature implements the Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) and the Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO), as formulated by Marcos López de Prado.The DSR addresses the "Multiple Testing Problem." If you test 10,000 random strategies, the best one will look great by pure chance. The DSR corrects for this by "deflating" the observed Sharpe Ratio based on:The number of trials conducted ($N$).The variance of the results across those trials.The skewness and kurtosis of the return distribution.The formula for the DSR is derived from the False Strategy Theorem, which calculates the expected maximum Sharpe Ratio for a set of random, unskilled strategies. If your strategy's DSR is high, it means its performance is statistically significant even after accounting for the fact that you searched through many possibilities to find it.Complementing the DSR is the Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV) method for calculating PBO. The CSCV procedure divides the backtest into $S$ slices and creates $C_{S, S/2}$ combinations of training and testing sets. PBO is defined as the probability that the strategy that was "optimal" in the training sets will underperform the average strategy in the testing sets. A high PBO is a definitive warning that the strategy has been "overfit" to the past and will likely fail in live trading.6. Trade Simulator and Transaction Cost ModelingThe "Edge" is only useful if it can survive the "friction" of real-world trading. Most strategies that look good on paper are destroyed by spreads, slippage, and fees.6.1 Layman’s Explanation: The Cost of Doing BusinessImagine you see a shirt on sale for $10 at a store that is 50 miles away. If it costs you $15 in gas to get there, the shirt isn't actually a deal—it’s a loss. In trading, the "gas" is the spread (the difference between the buy and sell price) and the fees you pay to your broker. Our trade simulator is "pessimistic"—it assumes you get the worst possible price and pay the highest fees, just to see if your strategy is strong enough to still make a profit.6.2 Professional Analysis: Transaction Cost Analysis (TCA) and TradeabilityThe Trade Simulator implements a sophisticated cost model that moves beyond simple flat-fee assumptions. For options, transaction costs are highly sensitive to the current volatility environment and the time to expiration.The total round-trip cost is modeled as:$$\text{Cost} = \text{Spread} \cdot Q + 2 \cdot \text{Slippage} \cdot \text{Mid} \cdot Q + 2 \cdot \text{Commissions} \cdot Q$$A key innovation in the Edge feature is the Tradeability Flag. Every simulated trade is evaluated against a liquidity floor. If a trade requires buying more contracts than the average daily volume allows, or if the bid-ask spread is more than 25% of the option's value, the trade is flagged as "Theoretical". This prevents the user from being "fooled" by strategies that only work on paper using illiquid, untradable instruments.6.3 Comprehensive Reference: The Madhavan-Smidt Spread ModelThe options spread model is based on the Madhavan-Smidt liquidity framework, which links the bid-ask spread to the inventory risk and asymmetric information costs faced by market makers.The empirical spread is estimated using the formula:$$\text{Spread}(K, T, \sigma) = \max\left(0.05, \; k \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{T} \cdot \left(1 + \alpha \cdot |\Delta - 0.5|\right) \cdot S\right)$$This model captures three critical market dynamics:Volatility Scaling ($\sigma$): Market makers widen spreads when volatility is high to compensate for the increased risk of a price swing during the time they hold the contract.Time Scaling ($\sqrt{T}$): The longer the time to maturity, the greater the uncertainty, leading to wider spreads. This follows the "square root of time" rule commonly used in risk scaling.Moneyness Bias ($\Delta$): Options that are deep out-of-the-money or deep in-the-money (where delta is far from 0.5) are less liquid and have higher relative spreads compared to at-the-money options.By using this data-driven baseline, the simulator provides a realistic "pessimistic-first" view of strategy performance. Research indicates that the square-root law of market impact holds across almost every asset class, from stocks to options to bitcoin. The Edge feature ensures that this fundamental law is respected, providing a "net" return that is achievable in live markets.7. The Edge Score: A Bayesian Composite SignalThe culmination of the Edge feature is the Edge Score, a composite indicator that summarizes all the quantitative research views into a single, actionable number.7.1 Layman’s Explanation: The "Confidence Meter"The Edge Score is like a "Confidence Meter" for a trade. It looks at the VRP (is it a good deal?), the Regimes (is the weather right?), and the Trends (is the market moving the right way?). If all these signals agree that it’s a great time to buy, the meter goes to +1. If they all say it’s a dangerous time, it goes to -1. If they disagree, it stays at 0. It’s a simple way to see if all the complex math is pointing in the same direction.7.2 Professional Analysis: Normalized Multi-Factor AggregationThe Edge Score ($E_t$) is a weighted sum of four sub-scores, each normalized to a $[-1, 1]$ range.$$E_t = w_1 S^{VRP}_t + w_2 S^{Regime}_t + w_3 S^{IV}_t + w_4 S^{Trend}_t$$$S^{VRP}$: Measures the current Variance Risk Premium. High positive VRP = high score for short-volatility edges.$S^{Regime}$: A user-defined mapping that assigns a score to each market regime (e.g., "Trending Bull" = 0.8, "Volatile Bear" = -0.5).$S^{IV}$: The percentile of IV30 over the last 252 days. High IV percentiles often signal "peaked" fear and a coming mean-reversion.$S^{Trend}$: The strength of the current trend, normalized by ATR. Strong trends generally penalize long-volatility strategies due to the high cost of gamma hedging.7.3 Comprehensive Reference: Bayesian Weights and Tanh NormalizationTo ensure the Edge Score is robust against outliers, each component is passed through a hyperbolic tangent ($\tanh$) activation function. This "squashes" extreme values, ensuring that no single factor can completely dominate the score unless others are also supportive.Furthermore, the system allows for the calibration of the weights ($w_i$) using a Bayesian approach. Instead of assuming the weights are fixed, the system can estimate them based on the historical "hit rate" of each component in the current market regime. For example, in a "Low Vol Trending" regime, the Trend Score might be given a higher weight, while in a "High Vol Choppy" regime, the VRP Score becomes the dominant factor.To prevent overfitting of the Edge Score itself, performance is always measured using the Deflated Sharpe Ratio on the "tradable-only" net equity curve produced by the Trade Simulator. This "Anti-Overfitting Rail" ensures that the user is not just finding a set of weights that worked in the past, but a genuine, statistically significant edge.8.
